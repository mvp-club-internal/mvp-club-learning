Get Clear on Your Goal

By the end of this hour you'll have a project workspace, a conversation that reduced the ambiguity around a real idea, your first intent and success criteria written into a docs/ folder, and a post in the channel.

Before we start: have Claude Code installed and open (the setup steps live on the home page), and make sure you're in the AI Summer Camp cohort channel in the MVP Club community. That's where we share prompts and answer questions live.
1

You'll feel behind — and here's what we do about it

we hand you the durable skills, not the weekly churn

Let's name it honestly: almost everyone in this cohort feels behind right now. AI is a technology that changes by the week. A new model, a new tool, a new "you have to try this" lands every few days. That pace is real, and it isn't going to ease up.

But underneath the churn, something steadier is taking shape: a set of core skills and durable mental models that hold even as the specific tools change. How to turn a fuzzy idea into a clear goal. How to scope and document before you build. How to direct an AI instead of being directed by it. These are what orient you in a moment of rapid disruption.

That is exactly what this camp develops and practices. We're not promising the churn will slow down. We're handing you the skills that outlast it, so the next new tool becomes something you pick up in an afternoon instead of something that leaves you behind. Today we start with the most foundational skill of all: getting clear on what you actually want before you ask for it.

2

The one big idea: Claude is guessing — your job is to shape the guess

more shared context moves the likely answer toward what you actually meant

Here is the mental model that everything else this summer rests on. A large language model is a probabilistic system. When you give it a prompt, it doesn't look up an answer. It predicts the most likely continuation, a kind of weighted average of everything it has seen that fits what you said. Claude is, in a real sense, always guessing. Your job is to shape the guess.

The way you shape it is by reducing ambiguity. A vague prompt leaves the likely answer sitting on a generic average, the response that fits the largest number of possible intentions. The more relevant context you provide, the narrower the range of likely answers becomes, until the most probable output is the specific thing you had in mind. Inputs in, output out. Better inputs, sharper output.

English is the new abstraction layer

Step back and notice what this changes. For decades, telling a computer what to do meant climbing down an abstraction ladder: English in your head, then Python or JavaScript, then assembly, then machine code, each rung further from how you actually think. An LLM collapses that. It is the translation of human intent into digital systems. English is the new abstraction layer for building and running software. The clearer your English, the better the system you get, which is why learning to express intent precisely is now a builder's core skill.

Read it left to right: with little to go on, the likely answer is a wide, flat distribution sitting on a generic average. As you stack context (your prompt, the background you share, and the files in your directory the model can read), the distribution narrows and its peak slides over to the answer you actually meant.

Each layer in that stack is a way to add relevant context: the prompt you write, the context you share around it, and the directory of files Claude can read. Giving the model the right context, deliberately and through back-and-forth dialogue, has a name. It's called context engineering, and effective prompting through dialogue is exactly that. The rest of this session is your first real practice of it.

Step 1 · Create a place to work

files and folders, all the way down

Here is a fact that takes the mystery out of building software: everything on your computer is files and folders. You already work this way every day. A document, a spreadsheet, a slide deck: each is a file, and you keep them in folders so you can find them again.

Software is no different. A website is just files sitting in folders on a server, telling your browser what to show. An app, an agent, a workflow: same story underneath. The only thing that changes when you work with Claude is that you operate over a wider range of files than you're used to (HTML, CSS, and JavaScript for a web page; Python for a script; whatever the job needs). The good news is that you don't have to author any of them. You describe what you want, and Claude translates that intent into the right files.

So your job isn't to write code. Your job is to keep a sensible structure over the files and folders, and to manage Claude as it works inside that structure: control what it reads so it understands your inputs, look at what it produces, feed corrections back, and go again. That loop is the same whether you're building an app, an agent, or a workflow.

A project folder on your Desktop
~/Desktop/wardrobe-app/your workspace
└── docs/
└── (your intent & success criteria land here next)

One folder, named for your project, with room for a docs/ folder inside it. That's the whole structure to start. You'll write your first document into docs/ later in this session.

Choosing a folder and pointing Claude at it is your first act of context engineering. You've drawn a boundary around the work: this is the place where shared context will collect, and the only files Claude can see are the ones you put here. A clean workspace is the start of a clean conversation.

Step 2 · Have the conversation

get Claude to interview you

Now the part that feels backwards at first: don't ask Claude to build anything yet. The opening move is "Here's my idea, let's talk." You point Claude at your idea and have it ask you the questions, the way a good product manager would, until your intent and your idea of success are out in the open where you can both see them.

Most ideas start fuzzy, and that's fine. The conversation is how the fog clears. Watch how it goes with a real example: someone who wants help with their clothes.

How the conversation unfolds

It usually opens vague, gets interrogated, and ends sharp:

A worked example

You: "I want help with my clothes. Something about my style, I guess. I never know what to buy."

Claude: "Before we build anything, a few questions. When you say 'style,' is the goal to look like a specific person or vibe you admire? And what's gone wrong before: buying the wrong things, or buying good things that wear out fast?"

You: "Both, honestly. I see people who dress well and want that, but the stuff I buy either doesn't match or falls apart. And I can't spend a fortune."

Claude: "So success isn't 'more clothes.' It sounds like: figure out a look you admire, find a version that fits you, and buy pieces that last without overspending. Should durability outweigh price when they conflict?"

You: "Yes. I'd rather pay a bit more for something that lasts, as long as it still looks right."

Notice what happened. You didn't arrive with a clear spec; the questions pulled it out of you. By the end you can state the idea in a single paragraph:

The sharpened idea: analyze the wardrobes of people whose style I admire, find a comparable style that works for me, then search for items on a price-to-quality matrix. Weight it toward durability, because I want clothes that last, but stay price-sensitive, and it has to look good.

That paragraph is the destination of Step 2. It names your intent (a durable, affordable wardrobe modeled on people you admire) and what success looks like (the price-to-quality tradeoff, weighted toward durability). Those success criteria are how you'll later judge whether Claude's work is any good.

Try it · drive the interview with your own idea

Tell Claude your idea in whatever rough shape it's in, then use these moves one at a time. Don't paste them all at once: send one, read the questions, answer honestly, then reach for the next.

Interview me like a product manager about this idea before we build anything. Ask me one question at a time.
What's ambiguous or underspecified about what I just told you? List the gaps.
Reflect back my intent and what success looks like in one paragraph, and flag everywhere you're still guessing.

Every question Claude asks and you answer is context added to the conversation. You're narrowing the guess on purpose, the same move as the bell curve in section 2, except here you do it through dialogue instead of one long prompt.

Step 3 · Use Claude to push your thinking

pressure-test the idea before you commit to it

Once the idea is sharp, you can use Claude to make it stronger. Left to its own defaults, Claude tends to agree with you. The most likely continuation of "here's my plan" is "great plan, with a few minor considerations." You get a better partner by assigning it a role and asking it to be critical on purpose.

Here's a menu of moves. You don't need all of them. Pick the lens that fits where your idea feels shaky, run it, and feed what you learn back into your one-paragraph statement.

Move 1 · The market-research lens

Have Claude research the space: who else solves this, and whether there's real demand. This grounds your idea in what already exists instead of in your assumptions.

Research the space around my idea. Who already solves this, what do they do well or badly, and is there real demand? Cite what you base each point on.

Move 2 · The workflow-optimization lens

Ask for the highest-leverage path to the goal, not the first one that comes to mind. You're looking for the shortest route to something useful.

Given my goal and success criteria, what's the highest-leverage path to a useful first version? What's the smallest thing worth building first, and what can wait?

Move 3 · The "senior engineer" lens

Give Claude a specific role, specific criteria, and a specific output format. Vague critique gets vague answers; a defined review gets a real one.

Review my plan as a senior engineer would before a real build. Evaluate the approach for hidden complexity, things that will break as it grows, and dependencies I'd regret. List each concern with how serious it is and what you'd do instead.

Move 4 · The pre-mortem lens

Imagine the project already failed, then work backward to why. This surfaces the assumptions you're leaning on without noticing.

Imagine it's three months from now and this project quietly failed. Write the story of why. What assumptions did I make that turned out wrong, and what would have warned me early?
Say you're not technical. Tell Claude plainly: "I'm not technical. For every decision, explain the consequences in plain language so I can weigh the tradeoffs." This isn't a disclaimer, it's a thinking-pusher in its own right. It forces the tradeoffs out into the open, in words a beginner can actually judge, which is exactly the ambiguity-reduction move this whole session is about.

Pushing your thinking is still context engineering. Each lens hands Claude a different vantage point, and each round of critique sharpens the shared picture of what you're building and why. By the end you'll have an idea that has survived a few good arguments, which is a much better thing to write down than a hunch.

Step 4 · Document it

turn the conversation into something durable

Right now your sharpened idea lives in a chat. Close the window and it's gone. The last move of this session is to make it permanent: have Claude write your intent and your success criteria into a file, inside a docs/ folder in your project. A conversation is fleeting. A file stays, and Claude can read it next week to pick up exactly where you left off.

This is also the cleanest example yet of the one big idea. When you create a docs/ folder and put your goal in it, you've literally added a new input to your workspace. Next time you ask Claude to build, that file is part of what it reads. You are shaping the guess by hand, one file at a time.

Same folder as section 3, one file fuller. The docs/ folder is no longer a placeholder — your intent and success criteria live in intent.md, and that file is now part of what Claude reads.

Try it · write your goal into a file

Create a docs/ folder and write a file called intent.md that captures my intent and what success looks like, in plain language. Use what we worked out in this conversation.
Now start a draft PRD in docs/ — a short product requirements doc that turns intent.md into goals, a first feature, and how we'll know it's working. Keep it rough; we'll refine it before next session.

This file is your on-ramp to Week 2. You'll walk into the next session with a real document instead of a vague memory, and Claude will read it to get its bearings instantly. Writing intent down is the difference between starting over each week and building something that compounds.

Before next week

deepen your project & add your power-ups

Homework, but the good kind: you're not doing a throwaway exercise, you're continuing the exact project you started today. The goal is to walk into Session 2 with a clearer idea and a draft PRD already written. Here's how to get there.

1 · Keep the conversation going

Go back to your own project and keep refining. Tighten your intent, sharpen your success criteria, and let Claude research the space and pressure-test your thinking using the lenses from section 5. The more you reduce the ambiguity now, the less you'll wrestle with it when you start building.

One built-in worth trying: Plan Mode. Instead of having Claude act right away, ask it to plan an approach first. It will lay out the steps it would take and, importantly, the assumptions it's making. Those assumptions are exactly where hidden ambiguity hides — read them, correct the ones that are off, and you've reduced the guess before any work happens.

Enter Plan Mode and plan an approach for my project based on docs/intent.md. List your assumptions explicitly so I can correct the ones that are wrong before we build.

2 · Add a couple of skills — your power-ups

This part is optional. If you skip it, you'll still arrive next week with a PRD, which is what matters. But if you have ten extra minutes, two skills make the work easier.

A skill is reusable, packaged context that aligns Claude automatically. Instead of explaining what you want from scratch every time, a skill hands Claude the right background and instructions the moment it's relevant. It's the Day-1 thesis (better inputs, sharper output) leveled up and made reusable. For the fuller explainer, see what is a skill? from the agents lesson on this hub.

The easiest way to add skills is the interactive menu. In Claude Code, run:

/plugin

That opens the plugin marketplace. Browse it, find the two skills below by name, and install them. (Each name has a @claude-plugins-official tag — that's the marketplace they come from, and what you'll look for in the menu.)

playground  ·  playground@claude-plugins-official
A quick interactive explorer that lets you see and tweak what "success" looks like, visually, before you commit to it.
superpowers  ·  superpowers@claude-plugins-official
Unlocks two moves you'll use a lot: brainstorming (turns your idea into a spec or PRD through guided back-and-forth) and deep-research (real market and competitor research on your idea).

3 · Draft the PRD into docs/

However you get there, whether by hand or by running the brainstorming skill (which ends by writing a spec for you), the finish line for this week is a draft PRD sitting in your docs/ folder. Lock that in and you walk into Session 2 ready to build instead of ready to start thinking.

Everything this week is the same skill you practiced today: reduce ambiguity, write it down, let the files shape the next guess. Do it on your own project and Session 2 starts from a running start.
