The Four Moves of Knowledge Work with AI

Matt · MVP Club

In the next 20–30 minutes you'll learn a simple, tool-agnostic model for getting genuinely good work out of any AI chat — then watch it run, move for move, across three everyday jobs: drafting comms, managing a project, and doing research.

Data

Intent

Instructions
how to do it
Evaluation

This model is our distillation of Nate B Jones' approach to knowledge work with AI — the idea that good output comes from preparing the sources, defining what you're making, constraining how it's made, and verifying it like a skeptical reviewer.

✦

Why your AI starts from zero

every new chat is a blank slate

AI doesn't look up the right answer the way you might hope. It predicts the most likely continuation of what you put in front of it — a kind of weighted average of everything that fits. And every new chat starts from zero: it knows nothing about your situation except what's in the box.

So the quality of the answer depends almost entirely on the quality of the input. Vague in, vague out. A one-line request gets a generic guess. A rich, well-aimed request gets something you can actually use. The whole skill is learning what to put in the box.

The one idea to hold onto: AI is always guessing. Your job is to shape the guess. There are four moves that do the shaping — and they're the same four whether you're writing an email, planning a project, or researching a decision.

◆

The four moves

one loop · Data → Intent → Instructions → Evaluation

Four moves turn a vague request into a strong one. Make all four and the guess gets sharp. Skip one and you can usually feel exactly which one is missing.

The raw material you hand it — context, files, examples.

Drafting comms

the four moves, applied to rallying volunteers for an event
The scenario
Emailing volunteer groups about an upcoming event — and the details are scattered across your planning docs and playbooks.
DataPaste the playbook plus this event's date, place, roles, and what to bring.
IntentVolunteers arrive knowing their role and what to expect — not just that an event is happening.
InstructionsWarm, skimmable; cover role, logistics, and what to bring; end with one clear RSVP ask.
EvaluationBefore showing me: real specifics from my docs, not filler? What would a first-timer still have to ask?
See / copy the full prompt 
You're helping me write an email inviting a volunteer group to an
upcoming event.

DATA:
[paste the relevant planning-doc / playbook sections + this event's details]
- Event: [what it is, date, time, location].
- What volunteers will actually do: [roles / tasks].
- Audience: [which group], who [already know us / are brand new].

INTENT:
After reading, each volunteer should know exactly what they'll be doing and
what to expect on the day — so they show up prepared and I get fewer
follow-up questions. Pull the specifics for THIS event and THIS group out of
the material above; don't speak in generalities.

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Warm, concrete, skimmable. Short paragraphs or a few bullets.
- Cover: their role, logistics (when / where), what to bring, who to find on arrival.
- End with one clear ask: confirm by [date] / RSVP here.
- Don't invent details that aren't in my material — if something's missing,
  leave a [BRACKET] for me to fill.

EVALUATION — before showing me:
- Would a first-time volunteer know what to do and what to expect?
- Did you use the real specifics from my docs, or generic filler?
- List anything they'd still have to email me to ask.
What evaluation catches
⚠Generic "come join us!" filler instead of the real role and logistics from your playbook.
⚠Invented times or locations that aren't in your material.
⚠Gaps a first-timer trips on — what to bring, where to go, who to find.
2

Project management

the same four moves, turning a sprawling event into a plan you won't drop
The scenario
You're running an event — a drive to add people to a registry you manage. Tasks to finish, supplies to bring, people to coordinate, and nothing can slip.
DataDump all my notes plus the event date, location, goal (40 sign-ups), and past runs.
IntentA plan I can run the day from — nothing forgotten. Not a summary of my notes.
InstructionsThree tracks — tasks to finish · things to bring · people to coordinate — sequenced by date.
EvaluationBefore showing me: what did I forget? Are the "bring" and "contact" lists complete enough to run the day?
See / copy the full prompt 
Turn everything I'm juggling for this event into one plan I won't drop
anything from.

DATA:
[dump all your notes, to-dos, and reminders here — unstructured is fine]
- Event: a drive to add people to a registry I manage.
- Date / location: [...]. My goal: [e.g. 40 sign-ups].
- Past runs / playbook notes: [paste if you have them].

INTENT:
I need a plan I can actually run the event from — everything I have to do
before, everything I have to bring, and everyone I have to coordinate with —
organized so nothing slips. Not a summary of my notes.

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Organize into three tracks: (1) Tasks to finish before, (2) Things to bring on
  the day, (3) People to contact / coordinate.
- Within each, sequence by when it has to happen ("2 weeks out", "day before",
  "morning of").
- Mark anything I didn't specify as "UNKNOWN — needs confirming". Don't invent it.

EVALUATION — before showing me:
- What's commonly needed for an event like this that I forgot to mention? List it
  separately under "you may be missing...".
- Are the "bring" and "coordinate" lists complete enough to run the day?
- What is the single next thing I should do today?
What evaluation catches
⚠Things you simply forgot to mention — the prompt asks Claude to surface them.
⚠A "bring" or "contact" list that looks done but misses a day-of essential.
⚠Invented tasks or deadlines presented as if they came from you.

Research

the same four moves — with evaluation doing the heavy lifting
The scenario
You want to grow your volunteer base in a region — systematically finding the people and groups who fit, and how to reach out and invite them.
DataThe region plus the persona I want — who they are, their groups, why they'd care — and what collaboration means.
IntentA systematic, actionable shortlist I can work this week — not a general overview of the area.
InstructionsGroup candidates by type; why each fits; a contact path and tailored opener; flag what to verify.
EvaluationBefore showing me: my persona or generic? Flag every invented or unverified name and contact.
See / copy the full prompt 
Help me systematically find volunteers in a region — and how to reach them.

DATA:
- Region: [city / area].
- The persona I'm looking for: [who they are, groups they belong to, what would
  make them care].
- What collaborating with me looks like: [volunteering at registry drives, etc.].
- Constraints: [time commitment, travel, anything disqualifying].

INTENT:
Give me a systematic shortlist of groups and people in this region who fit the
persona, plus how to approach each one — something I can act on this week,
not a general overview of the area.

INSTRUCTIONS:
- Group candidates by type (community orgs, faith groups, campus clubs,
  workplaces, etc.). For each: why they fit, a contact path, and a one-line
  opener tailored to them.
- Separate what you actually know from what you're inferring.
- Flag every name, contact, or claim I should verify myself before reaching out.

EVALUATION — before showing me:
- Did you use my persona and region, or fall back on generic suggestions?
- Is anything — org names, contacts, facts — invented or unverified?
  Mark each one clearly so I don't email a dead end.
What evaluation catches
⚠Plausible-sounding org names or contacts that don't actually exist.
⚠Generic "reach out to local nonprofits" advice instead of your specific persona.
⚠Inferences about a group's interest stated as if they were established fact.

Recap

four moves, any job

Across a volunteer email, an event plan, and a regional research sweep, the loop never changed. That's the whole point — it's portable. Learn the four moves once and you have a method for almost anything you'd hand to an AI.

Data
Put the real material in the box. If it isn't there, it doesn't exist.
Intent
Name the job the output has to do, not just its topic.
Instructions
Say the quiet part — format, constraints, what to avoid.
Evaluation
Decide what "good" means and make the model check before you read.
If a result disappoints you, don't retype it louder — ask which of the four moves you skipped. It's almost always one of them.
